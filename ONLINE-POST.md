## Short (LinkedIn / Bluesky / similar)

AI: fantastic assistant, terrible decision-maker.

After a couple of years of this, the pattern is obvious: **value is less about “doing the work” and more about knowing what work to do**—and how to steer the tools so they stay pointed at the right problem.

The model will execute generously. It will also burn your calendar on the wrong brief if nobody is holding the line.

**Leadership sets priorities and staffing—not technology.** The “discount” from automation shows up as time you can reinvest in research, context, audience, and what actually ships.

## Slightly longer (if the network rewards a story beat)

I’ve been building with AI long enough to stop treating it like magic and start treating it like a very fast, very eager teammate.

**It’s a fantastic assistant and a weak decision-maker.** It answers the prompt you gave—not always the outcome you needed. So the job shifts: less hero coding, more framing, constraints, and review. Less “did it generate text,” more “did we pick the right problem.”

That’s not doom for developers; it’s a different emphasis. **Judgment, context, and accountability** matter more, not less. **Leadership determines layoffs and roadmaps—not the stack.**

I still ship small, honest experiments (a canvas game in plain JS, no asset pipeline, GitHub Pages) as a reminder: automation subsidizes execution; **someone still has to own intent.**
